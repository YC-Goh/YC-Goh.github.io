Note on Double Standards

Last Updated: 13 November 2023.

Introduction

I was looking for some information about what exactly constituted the charge of double standards against Israel recently and came across one of the few websites/articles that argued that Israel benfits from double standards, as opposed to the usual argument that Israel is unfairly held to double standards. Interesting as this piece is, I am not entirely convinced by this argument because it seems too convoluted. The charge of double standards to dismiss an argument seems to be wrong at a deeper level that ought to require an easier explanation. Having been reading up more and coming up short-handed and thinking a little too much about a topic that may not necessarily benefit me in the long run, this living note will serve to collect my thoughts on this topic.

What I Do Not Find Convincing

I preface this section by noting that the Mondoweiss article is ancient. This author may no longer stand by this article given recent events anyway so I might just be talking to myself at a wall. This article argues against the charge of double standards with respect to advocating for the Boycott, Divestment and Sanctions movement (BDS) using 3 claims:

  1. Advocating for BDS is the right thing to do when the call is made by the group being oppressed with legitimate grievances. This is not a double standard against Israel because the author would just as gladly advocate for BDS call by oppressed groups in any other country or region.
  2. Advocating for BDS while at least not advocating against (if not outright ignoring) other (supposedly) greater human rights abuses around the world is not a double standard because every human and organisation has limited time, attention and energy to spend towards advocating for the causes they care about most. Hence this framing of double standards is simply bizarre since everyone cares about different things and all concerns are equally legitimate even the vast majority of them are not concerned with the worst abuses or the peoples with greatest suffering.
  3. (This is a steelman argument) Even if we concede that everybody advocating for BDS against Israel is in fact guilty of double standards, this still does not automatically yield the conclusion that BDS advocates are antisemites. The proper conclusion is that the BDS advocate is against Israel (or most likely against specific Israeli policies), or to stretch the evidence against Zionism. However the stretch from being against Zionism to Jew-hatred is potentially too far. Zionism is Jewish Nationalism and Israel is the Jewish state that is the physical manifestation of Zionism. Conflating criticisms of Israel and/or Zionism with outright Jew-hatred is to conflate criticism of a political ideology with hatred of the people that that political ideology claims to represent (which those peoples may not even agree with). This point is crucial since the implicit claim is that criticisms of Israel by antisemites are motivated by blind hatred for Jews and therefore illegitimate (and ignorable).

Essentially, I find arguments 1 and 3 convoluted because it goes to great lengths to justify that the author would have applied the same standards to other similar cases, but for those similar cases actually existing beyond hypotheticals. This way of utilising the steelman argument implicitly cedes a point where critics applying double standards are in fact illegitimate, especially if the double standards are motivated by animus rather than a genuine concern for the (suppoesd) abuse at hand. I think this is wrong because this is essentially a form of shooting the messenger.

The Double Standards Logic

As I understand Double Standards, it makes the following argument:

  1. A call to action by person or organisation OOO on some issue III of perceived severity S(I)S(I)S(I) is morally legitimate only insofar as:
    • OOO has similarly called to action on at least some issues with greater perceived severity {i:i∈I,S(i)>S(I)}\{i:i\in\mathcal{I},S(i)>S(I)\}{i:i∈I,S(i)>S(I)}.
    • OOO is motivated by "genuine" concern for issue III such that OOO in "good faith" believes that issue III exists and is at least as severe as perceived.

How this argument is used against critics seems to proceed as follows:

  1. A caller to action OOO who has not similarly called to action on other issues of greater severity is unlikely to be motivated by genuine belief in the severity of the current issue.
  2. Since OOO is unlikely to be motivated by genuine belief, it follows that OOO is therefore likely to be motivated by nonbenevolent motives toward the target of the actions being called.
  3. Since OOO is likely to be motivated by nonbenevolent motives, the issue is unlikely to be genuine.
  4. Since the issue is unlikely to be genuine, the call to action is illegitimate on balance and must not be heeded lest an innocent party be harmed.

Most steps are couched in probabilistic terms as arguing against an absolute version of this argument would likely be a commission of the strawman argument fallacy. If this entire argument seems made-up and weaker than it should be, then one has to apologise because I kind of had to reverse-engineer it from how I see such arguments play out in public debate. Wanting to see the structure of this argument was why I was searching the web for rigorous definitions of Double Standards against Israel in the first place (and of the Double Standards argument more generally), but to not much avail.

Attacking the Logic

After laying it out, I think the links to attack are logically the first and third steps.

1. Questioning the Genuineness of Beliefs of the Caller

This step of the argument is not actually any kind of logic or step in a logical argument inasmuch as it is an accusation of intent. It sets a high moral standard for critics and then implies nonbenevolent motives to critics who do not meet the bar without explicitly ascribing those motives (i.e., the reader is invited to make the link themselves, to absolve the debater the responsibility of having to make the accusation).

For any person or organisation OOO to make calls to actions on some issue III, it is necessary that:

  1. OOO would gain sufficient utility from seeing progress being made toward a satisfactory resolution on III (or in other words, OOO cares about III). Note that actual resolution should not be necessary. This would be an artificially high bar since many issues that are sufficiently worthy of more widespread attention are deeply complex and cannot be resolved on any temporal scale other than the long run.
  2. There exists some form of action that is feasible and which OOO has reason to believe can, in sufficient probability, lead to progress toward a satisfactory resolution on III. This is self-explanatory because one cannot call for action on an issue if no feasible action to make progress towards a resolution exists.
  3. OOO has sufficient resources, including time and attention, to pursue effective advocacy toward such action that can reasonably lead to progress toward satisfactory resolution of III.

This is common sense, at least to those trained either in utilitarian philosophy or economics. Time, effort, and other resources are not in infinite supply. Therefore, one needs to focus narrowly on key issues that one sufficiently cares about with feasible actions in order to make any kind of progress.

This provides a framework for deconstructing the attempt to question the genuine beliefs of the caller:

  1. A caller of action on issue III may not be calling for action on many other (or even any other) issues of equal or greater perceived severity because doing so would inefficiently spread their resources too wide, resulting in inefficiency in achieving their goals for pursuing advocacy in the first place.
  2. A caller of action on issue III is in fact not obliged at all to care about any other issue regardless of severity if either feasible effective action does not exist (e.g., a hypothetical civil society-led BDS call against China) or the resolution or progress towards such resolution for those issues are meaningless for the caller.

To claim otherwise that any caller to action on some issue III is obliged to also advocate for action on all issues with greater severity than III or otherwise to risk the legitimacy of their advocacy is to ignore both how the real world operates as well as the fact that by the diversity of human preferences, someone else will be sufficiently interested in those other issues and advocate towards their resolution (i.e., division and specialisation of labour in advocacy).

3. Questioning the Genuineness of the Issue

This step is in fact an informal fallacy. The unstated premise which leads to the conclusion that the issue is unlikely to be genuine is that the only relevant evidence for the claim of the existence of the issue is the reputation or history of the caller. Notice that the reputation of the caller is at best tangential to the existence of the issue unless it is the only relevant piece of information available to the debaters (and audience), in which case then Bayesian logic concludes that the case for the existence of the issue is only as strong as subjective belief in the truthfulness of the caller.

Consider the following.

  1. Suppose that the caller is in fact biased against the target of the action being called. From the perspective of the caller, is it more efficient to invent a non-existent issue, or to call attention to a genuine issue with objective evidence?
  2. Suppose objective evidence on the existence of the issue exists. Then, how much consideration should one actually give to the identity of the messenger?

Stated plainly, this step in the Double Standards logic is suspect because at its root, it effectively claims that only actors not biased against the target are allowed to claim the existence of a genuine issue requiring action, where bias is defined arbitrarily as whether one cares equally for all or at least some other issues of at least equal perceived severity. Note the weight that effectively carries here because nowhere in this sequence of steps does it actually deny "biased" callers the right to call for action on that issue; instead this step of the Double Standards argument aims at casting sufficient doubt on the perceived goals of the caller such that debaters and the audience may prima facie dismiss the caller's argument without further examination, which effectively denies the caller the platform to make their case heard.

Questioning the Fundamental Premise

Consider the following.

  1. Suppose that the caller is in fact biased against the target of the action being called. Why would the caller be biased in the first place?

One could suspect that negative attitudes against a target normally neither arises out of thin air nor is entirely irrational. Many negative attitudes and biases can be explained (explanation does not imply moral agreement), even if one disagrees entirely with the causes of such biases.

For example, a state prosecutor for sex offences who regularly observes how much even the mildest of sex offences derails the lives of victims and how unrepentant most offenders are may rightly be expected to view sex offenders negatively in general and to advocate for stronger policy to prevent sex offences and to punish offenders. It does not matter that the prosecutor would be guilty of double standards by ignoring or not calling for action on crimes of greater severity elsewhere in the world (e.g., political arrests, torture, murder) because:

  1. Those are not within the prosecutor's scope of concern or expertise.
  2. It is inefficient for the prosecutor to advocate on issues where they have relevant knowledge.
  3. The prosecutor's advocacy is fundamentally premised not on their reputation but rather on the wealth of evidence on the existence of the issue being called out and efficacy of actions being called for.

Many (if not most) agents with negative bias toward another agent will have formed those biases due to either:

  1. Actual negative experiences in interactions with those agents (e.g., China and Southeast Asia; Ireland and the UK; the Middle East and the USA) that informs the biased agent that the target agent is capable of problematic behaviour that ought to be addressed.
  2. Information transmitted either culturally or through the media that informs the biased agent of problematic behaviours of the target agents.

The biasedness and completeness of the sources of information that biased agents use to form their views of target agents may be debatable, but biasedness of an agent per se ought never to be grounds to dismiss an argument or call to action as the source of those biases may in fact be directly relevant to the issue at hand being called.

Conclusion

The Double Standards argument is:

  1. An informal fallacy. It seeks to cast doubt on the trustworthiness of the messenger to dismiss the argument without engaging with the substance of the evidence for the existence of the issue and the associated call to action.
  2. A logical fallacy. It aims to lead debaters and the audience to behavioural conclusions about the caller using unstated premises about human behaviour that are unsupported by social and behavioural science.
  3. A deflection technique. It is a weapon to "win" public debates by subtly changing the question at hand from whether the issue exists and deserves to be acted upon to whether the caller has the right to call attention to this issue and the action.
